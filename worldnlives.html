AQA Worlds and Lives Poetry Anthology

Lines Written in Early Spring
William Wordsworth
Analysis & Context
I heard a thousand blended notes,

Harmony in nature: The auditory image reflects the unity and peace of the natural world, creating a sense of calm.

While in a grove I sate reclined,

Relaxation and setting: The poet's calm posture and the natural setting reflect a meditative and reflective tone.

In that sweet mood when pleasant thoughts

Emotional duality: The poet is in a gentle, reflective mood — the calm before his thoughts turn sorrowful.

Bring sad thoughts to the mind.

Foreshadowing sadness: Introduces a paradox: beauty causes grief, possibly because it reminds him of human failure.

To her fair works did Nature link

Personification: Nature is given a human quality, reinforcing Romantic ideals of nature's power and wisdom.

The human soul that through me ran;

Spiritual unity: Wordsworth feels a spiritual bond between nature and himself — nature inspires his inner life.

And much it grieved my heart to think

Emotional response: His sorrow deepens as he reflects on how humans have damaged this sacred connection.

What man has made of man.

Condemnation of society: A powerful line showing disillusionment with mankind's cruelty, especially during industrialisation and war.

Through primrose tufts, in that green bower,

Natural beauty: Vivid visual imagery celebrates delicate spring flowers, suggesting innocence and renewal.

The periwinkle trailed its wreaths;

Growth and connection: The trailing plant suggests interconnection and peaceful coexistence in the natural world.

And 'tis my faith that every flower

Romantic belief: The poet expresses his personal faith that nature is purposeful and morally uplifting.

Enjoys the air it breathes.

Personification: Flowers are shown as sentient, enjoying life — reinforcing nature’s vitality and joy.

The birds around me hopped and played,

Joy in nature: The playful birds reflect the innocence and freedom that nature offers.

Their thoughts I cannot measure:—

Humility: The speaker admits he doesn’t understand animals’ thoughts, showing respect and wonder.

But the least motion which they made

Mindfulness: Even the smallest actions of nature inspire deep thought and reflection in the poet.

It seemed a thrill of pleasure.

Emotional response: The birds’ movements seem joyous — reflecting the pure happiness found in nature’s simplicity.

The budding twigs spread out their fan,

Imagery of growth: This visual metaphor captures nature’s active flourishing in spring, full of hope and life.

To catch the breezy air;

Personification: The twigs ‘catching’ the air suggests they are alive and participating in spring’s renewal.

And I must think, do all I can,

Reflection: The poet insists on believing in the goodness of nature, even if it’s just a personal view.

That there was pleasure there.

Affirmation: Despite sorrow, he chooses to see happiness and peace in nature — a hopeful ending.

If this belief from heaven be sent,

Spiritual question: He wonders if this belief in nature’s joy is divinely inspired — adding a layer of faith.

If such be Nature's holy plan,

Sacredness: Nature is described as holy, suggesting it has a divine purpose and moral clarity.

Have I not reason to lament

Moral dilemma: If nature is good and pure, the poet feels justified in mourning human cruelty and corruption.

What man has made of man?

Powerful conclusion: Repeating the line drives home his deep sadness over mankind’s self-destruction — a final contrast with the harmony of nature.

England in 1819
Percy Bysshe Shelley
Analysis & Context
An old, mad, blind, despised, and dying king,—

Analysis: Shelley uses a string of harsh adjectives to paint King George III as unfit and pitiful, symbolising a failing monarchy.

Princes, the dregs of their dull race, who flow

Analysis: The royal family is described as the worst of their lineage — unworthy and without wisdom or purpose.

Through public scorn,—mud from a muddy spring,—

Analysis: Powerful metaphor that presents the aristocracy as filthy and corrupt, born from a decaying source.

Rulers who neither see, nor feel, nor know,

Analysis: Accusation that leaders are detached and ignorant of the people’s suffering — emotionally and intellectually blind.

But leech-like to their fainting country cling

Analysis: Simile compares rulers to parasites, draining life from a weak and suffering England.

Till they drop, blind in blood, without a blow,—

Analysis: Violent imagery predicts their downfall — they will perish, overwhelmed by bloodshed, even without resistance.

A people starved and stabbed in the untilled field,—

Analysis: The common people are dying from both hunger and violence. The field is "untilled," suggesting neglect and ruin.

An army, whom liberticide and prey

Analysis: The military is used not to protect, but to crush freedom (liberticide) and exploit the people.

Makes as a two-edged sword to all who wield,—

Analysis: Metaphor for violence backfiring — even those in power will suffer from the brutal system they maintain.

Golden and sanguine laws which tempt and slay;

Analysis: Laws are deceptive — they appear noble (golden) but cause harm (sanguine meaning bloody).

Religion Christless, Godless—a book sealed;

Analysis: Religion is criticised for being empty — devoid of true spirituality, and inaccessible like a sealed book.

A Senate—Time’s worst statute unrepealed,—

Analysis: Parliament is described as an outdated and unjust institution that remains unchanged and harmful.

Are graves, from which a glorious Phantom may

Analysis: Despite all the decay, Shelley holds out hope — from these metaphorical graves, revolution (the "Phantom") may rise.

Burst, to illumine our tempestuous day.

Analysis: The poem ends with a hopeful vision of change — a bright future emerging from the chaos and oppression.

Shall Earth no More Inspire Thee
Emily Brontë
Analysis & Context
Shall earth no more inspire thee,

Analysis: The speaker asks if the natural world no longer offers inspiration, suggesting the addressee has become disconnected from nature’s beauty.

Thou lonely dreamer now?

Analysis: Describes the addressee as isolated and lost in thought, emphasizing their withdrawal from the world.

Since passion may not fire thee

Analysis: Indicates that the addressee’s emotional drive or enthusiasm has been extinguished, perhaps by sorrow or disillusionment.

Shall Nature cease to bow?

Analysis: Rhetorical question implying that nature remains constant in its beauty and willingness to comfort, even if the person turns away.

Thy mind is ever moving

Analysis: Suggests restlessness, as the addressee’s thoughts wander without purpose or peace.

In regions dark to thee;

Analysis: Metaphor for exploring unknown or troubling mental states, reflecting inner turmoil or depression.

Recall its useless roving—

Analysis: The speaker urges the addressee to recognize the futility of their aimless thoughts and return to the tangible world.

Come back and dwell with me.

Analysis: Invitation to rejoin the speaker and find solace in nature’s companionship rather than in isolation.

I know my mountain breezes

Analysis: Personifies nature’s elements as familiar friends, suggesting past comfort drawn from the speaker’s environment.

Enchant and soothe thee still—

Analysis: Emphasizes nature’s healing power, capable of easing grief and restoring peace to the mind.

I know my sunshine pleases

Analysis: Sunshine symbolizes warmth and joy, reinforcing the theme that nature offers emotional uplift.

Despite thy wayward will.

Analysis: Acknowledges the addressee’s resistance, yet insists that nature’s benevolence persists.

When day with evening blending

Analysis: Imagery of twilight suggests a transitional moment, mirroring change in mood from despair to hope.

Sinks from the summer sky,

Analysis: The seasonal setting evokes warmth and fullness, underpinning the comforting power of nature.

I’ve seen thy spirit bending

Analysis: Indicates the addressee’s spirit once responded to nature’s beauty, bowing in reverence or wonder.

In fond idolatry.

Analysis: The term “idolatry” underscores a deep admiration or almost worshipful attachment to nature’s beauty.

I’ve watched thee every hour;

Analysis: Shows the speaker’s attentive care and observation, highlighting the depth of their concern.

I know my mighty sway,

Analysis: Acknowledges nature’s powerful influence over human emotions and thoughts.

I know my magic power

Analysis: Repetition of “I know” creates a comforting certainty about nature’s ability to heal.

To drive thy griefs away.

Analysis: Concludes the stanza with the promise that nature can dispel sorrow and bring relief.

Few hearts to mortals given

Analysis: Suggests that not many people on earth are capable of deep, passionate feeling.

On earth so wildly pine;

Analysis: “Pine” as a verb conveys longing and yearning, reinforcing the theme of intense emotional need.

Yet none would ask a heaven

Analysis: Even with such deep longing, the addressee wouldn’t seek a perfect paradise beyond this world.

More like this earth than thine.

Analysis: Implies that the natural world around the addressee is so close to perfection that no other “heaven” could surpass it.

Then let my winds caress thee;

Analysis: Personifies the winds as tender companions, inviting comfort and intimacy.

Thy comrade let me be—

Analysis: The speaker offers friendship and support, emphasizing a desire for shared solace.

Since nought beside can bless thee,

Analysis: Asserts that nothing aside from nature’s embrace can bring the addressee true happiness.

Return and dwell with me.

Analysis: Final plea to reconnect with nature, framing it as a home where the addressee can find lasting peace.

In a London Drawingroom
George Eliot
Analysis & Context
The sky is cloudy, yellowed by the smoke.

Analysis: The polluted sky symbolises the environmental damage of industrialisation, creating a sense of oppression and decay.

For view there are the houses opposite

Analysis: The absence of open vistas emphasises urban enclosure and the monotony of the cityscape.

Cutting the sky with one long line of wall

Analysis: The continuous wall suggests the unbroken, confining nature of London’s architecture.

Like solid fog: far as the eye can stretch

Analysis: The simile compares buildings to fog, highlighting how the city smothers light and air.

Monotony of surface & of form

Analysis: Explicit mention of monotony underlines the uniform and unvarying character of urban life.

Without a break to hang a guess upon.

Analysis: The lack of variation offers no relief or focal point, reflecting emotional numbness.

No bird can make a shadow as it flies,

Analysis: Nature’s absence is stark; no bird’s shadow illustrates how lifeless the environment is.

For all is shadow, as in ways o'erhung

Analysis: The pervasive shadow imagery reinforces the oppressive atmosphere.

By thickest canvass, where the golden rays

Analysis: Any hint of warmth (“golden rays”) is covered by industrial by-products (“canvass”).

Are clothed in hemp. No figure lingering

Analysis: Industrial materials smother natural light; people do not pause to enjoy their surroundings.

Pauses to feed the hunger of the eye

Analysis: The lack of visual interest starves observers of aesthetic or emotional nourishment.

Or rest a little on the lap of life.

Analysis: There is no opportunity for rest or reflection, implying spiritual and emotional exhaustion.

All hurry on & look upon the ground,

Analysis: Passers-by avoid eye contact, indicating social isolation and lack of community.

Or glance unmarking at the passers by

Analysis: The fleeting glances show detachment and anonymity among city dwellers.

The wheels are hurrying too, cabs, carriages

Analysis: The hurried pace of vehicles mirrors the restless, mechanical rhythm of urban life.

All closed, in multiplied identity.

Analysis: The repeated “closed” suggests confinement, while “multiplied identity” highlights dehumanisation.

The world seems one huge prison-house & court

Analysis: Metaphor portrays the city as a place of punishment and confinement rather than freedom.

Where men are punished at the slightest cost,

Analysis: Even small mistakes incur harsh consequences, reinforcing the oppressive social environment.

With lowest rate of colour, warmth & joy.

Analysis: The lack of warmth and joy underscores the emotional and sensory deprivation of city life.

On an Afternoon Train from Purley to Victoria, 1955
James Berry
Analysis & Context
Hello, she said, and startled me.

Analysis: The casual greeting is unexpectedly direct, catching the speaker off guard and setting a tone of polite surprise.

Nice day.

Analysis: A simple, neutral comment that opens the conversation, illustrating customary small talk.

Nice day I agreed.

Analysis: The speaker’s echoing of her words shows politeness but also hints at limited engagement.

I am a Quaker she said and Sunday

Analysis: She shares her religious identity and motivation, introducing themes of faith and moral purpose.

I was moved in silence

Analysis: The speaker pauses respectfully, showing the solemnity of her spiritual experience.

to speak a poem loudly

Analysis: The contrast between silence and speaking loudly underscores the power of her conviction in racial brotherhood.

for racial brotherhood.

Analysis: Introduces the central theme of racial unity, reflecting post-war idealism.

I was thoughtful, then said

Analysis: Repetition of “thoughtful” highlights the speaker’s reflective pause, emphasizing introspection.

what poem came on like that?

Analysis: He questions her inspiration, revealing curiosity about her motivations.

One the moment inspired she said.

Analysis: Her reply connects creativity to spontaneity, suggesting art born from personal conviction.

I was again thoughtful.

Analysis: The speaker’s repeated pause emphasizes the weight of the dialogue and his careful listening.

Inexplicably I saw

Analysis: A sudden mental image emerges, indicating a deeper emotional response beyond mere words.

empty city streets lit dimly

Analysis: The imagery contrasts bleak urban life with the hope implied by her poem, highlighting displacement.

in a day’s first hours.

Analysis: Early morning light suggests new beginnings, yet the emptiness conveys loneliness.

Alongside in darkness

Analysis: The juxtaposition of light and darkness reflects his divided sense of belonging.

was my father’s big banana field.

Analysis: His affectionate memory of Jamaica contrasts with the drab London scene, underscoring themes of home and identity.

Where are you from? she said.

Analysis: A polite enquiry that reveals her genuine interest but also ignorance of Caribbean geography.

Jamaica I said.

Analysis: The straightforward answer asserts his identity, yet it prompts her next misunderstanding.

What part of Africa is Jamaica? she said.

Analysis: Her question exposes her lack of knowledge and common misconceptions about the Caribbean.

Where Ireland is near Lapland I said.

Analysis: His ironic retort highlights the absurdity of her assumption and uses humour to deflect awkwardness.

Hard to see why you leave

Analysis: She questions migration motives, showing empathy yet misunderstanding the complexities of exile.

such sunny country she said.

Analysis: The reference to Jamaica’s climate underscores superficial judgments about migration.

Snow falls elsewhere I said.

Analysis: His concise reply counters her remark, hinting at harsher realities he faced abroad.

So sincere she was beautiful

Analysis: He acknowledges her genuine kindness and warmth, bridging their cultural divide.

as people sat down around us.

Analysis: The gathering crowd suggests communal interest, emphasising the shared humanity revealed in conversation.

Name Journeys
Raman Mundair
Analysis & Context
Like Rama I have felt the Wilderness

Analysis: Invoking the figure of Rama immediately connects the speaker to exile and spiritual trial, suggesting their own journey is as profound and mythic.

but I have not been blessed

Analysis: Contrast word “but” highlights absence of support or “blessing,” underlining a solitary struggle.

with a companion as sweet as she,

Analysis: Reference to Sita as an ideal companion underscores the speaker’s sense of loss at not having such comforting presence.

Sita; loyal pure and true of heart.

Analysis: Listing of Sita’s virtues (alliteration on “p”) elevates her as a symbol of perfect loyalty—something the speaker yearns for.

Like her, I have been chastened

Analysis: Parallel structure with previous stanza ties the speaker’s suffering directly to Sita’s—reinforcing identification with the myth.

through trial by fire.

Analysis: “Fire” symbolizes purification but also pain, indicating that the speaker’s ordeal has a transformative yet harsh quality.

Sita and I,

Analysis: Direct address “Sita and I” collapses distance between myth and speaker, making the comparison immediate.

spiritual sari-sisters entwined

Analysis: Metaphor of “sari-sisters” and weaving (“entwined”) emphasizes cultural and spiritual kinship.

in an infinite silk that would swathe

Analysis: “Infinite silk” suggests an unbreakable bond; “swathe” conveys protection and comfort.

Draupadi’s blush.

Analysis: Reference to another mythological figure broadens the poem’s tapestry of cultural memory and emotional resonance.

My name

Analysis: Beginning new stanza with “My name” foregrounds the core theme of identity.

a journey between rough and smooth,

Analysis: Oxymoron “rough and smooth” captures the tensions in carrying a non-English name in Britain.

an interlacing of banyan leaves with sugar cane.

Analysis: Exotic natural imagery fuses strength (“banyan”) with sweetness (“sugar cane”), symbolising hybrid identity.

Woven tapestries of Journeys;

Analysis: Textile metaphor continues—identity as artful weaving of multiple cultural threads.

traveling from South

Analysis: Geographic movement from “South” evokes origin; line break suspends expectation, mirroring dislocation.

to North, where the Punjabi in my mouth

Analysis: “Punjabi in my mouth” personifies language as physical substance, highlighting its importance to identity.

became dislodged as milk teeth fell

Analysis: Childhood image (“milk teeth”) conveys involuntary loss of mother tongue—natural yet painful.

and hit infertile English soil.

Analysis: Soil metaphor underscores barrenness of English culture for non-native languages.

My mouth toiled to accommodate

Analysis: Personification of “mouth” depicts the effort and strain of pronunciation.

the rough musicality of Mancunian vows

Analysis: “Mancunian vows” anchors the poem in place (Manchester), contrasting regional English accent with the speaker’s own.

and my name became a stumble

Analysis: Metaphor for mispronunciation—identity awkwardly “stumbling” in a foreign tongue.

that filled English mouths

Analysis: Emphasises how the speaker’s name is the vessel for misunderstanding.

with a discordant rhyme, an exotic

Analysis: “Discordant rhyme” suggests ugliness or mismatch in English ears, “exotic” otherness.

rhythm dulled, my voice a mystery

Analysis: Loss of clarity—voice reduced to an unintelligible “mystery” in the cultural echo chamber.

in the Anglo Echo chamber—

Analysis: Powerful metaphor for English society’s insular repetition and non-recognition of difference.

void of history and memory.

Analysis: Final line condemns the lack of shared context, highlighting alienation.

A Wider View
Seni Seneviratne
Analysis & Context
I stood, a wetsuit stretched over empty bones,

Analysis: Simile extending emptiness into a “wetsuit”—protective shell over hollow core, evoking vulnerability.

eyes trained on horizons I could never reach,

Analysis: “Horizon” as unreachable goal—foreshadows disappointment in migration or aspiration.

the sky a tinted ceiling,

Analysis: Metaphor of sky as confined “ceiling” reverses expected openness of sky.

like a museum’s dimmed glass.

Analysis: “Museum glass” suggests preservation—but also barrier between observer and reality.

Below, glass buildings rose,

Analysis: Ironic mirror of sky—man-made structures replace natural expanse, reflecting—but isolating.

prisons of refracted light

Analysis: “Prisons” again—light (hope) is trapped, suggesting urban entrapment.

that bent my body to their will.

Analysis: Personification of buildings imposing on the speaker’s physical form—loss of autonomy.

And yet my heart wandered,

Analysis: “Yet” signals contrast—despite oppression, inner spirit remains free.

over lands I once called home,

Analysis: The memory of home offers emotional escape.

to smell the jasmine at my grandmother’s door,

Analysis: Sensory detail (“smell jasmine”) evokes vivid memory and cultural roots.

to taste the salt of seas I used to swim.

Analysis: Another sensory image ties identity to landscape and childhood.

I turned back then,

Analysis: Physical and emotional pivot—speaker chooses memory over current confinement.

and let the city keep its mirrored lies.

Analysis: “Mirrored lies” indict urban illusions of beauty and progress.
