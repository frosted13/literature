GCSE English Lit Flashcards

Macbeth
"Is this a dagger which I see before me"
Shows his hallucinations and descent into madness, indicating guilt before the murder of Duncan.
Macbeth
"I am in blood stepped in so far"
Symbolises his point of no return; he's too deep in his crimes to turn back now.
Macbeth
"Vaulting ambition"
He acknowledges ambition is his only motivator, which foreshadows his tragic downfall.
Lady Macbeth
"Unsex me here"
Wants to shed her femininity to gain strength to commit murder — challenges gender norms.
Lady Macbeth
"A little water clears us of this deed"
Shows her naivety thinking guilt can be washed away — ironic as she later breaks down.
Lady Macbeth
"Out, damned spot!"
Her guilt manifests as hallucinations — now she cannot wash it off like she once claimed.
Banquo
"Thou shalt get kings, though thou be none"
Witches prophesy his children will be kings, linking him to legacy and fate.
Banquo
"What, can the devil speak true?"
He is suspicious of the witches, showing his cautious, moral nature.
Banquo
"Fly, good Fleance, fly!"
Protects his son, shows his bravery and hope for the prophecy to be fulfilled through Fleance.
Macduff
"O horror, horror, horror!"
His genuine grief at Duncan’s murder shows his loyalty to Scotland.
Macduff
"All my pretty ones?"
Emotional reaction to the death of his family, humanises him and fuels his revenge.
Macduff
"Turn, hell-hound, turn!"
He directly confronts Macbeth, calling him evil — symbol of justice and vengeance.
The Witches
"Fair is foul, and foul is fair"
Theme of deception and inversion — reality is twisted by supernatural forces.
The Witches
"None of woman born shall harm Macbeth"
Deceptive prophecy giving Macbeth false confidence.
The Witches
"Double, double toil and trouble"
Witches' chant evokes chaos and sets a dark, sinister tone.
Sherlock Holmes
"My mind... rebels at stagnation."
Holmes thrives on mental stimulation and puzzles — shows his need for constant challenge.
Sherlock Holmes
"You know my methods. Apply them."
Highlights his logical approach and confidence in his own intellect.
Sherlock Holmes
"Detection is, or ought to be, an exact science"
His belief in rationality and method sets him apart from others.
Dr. Watson
"An oasis of art in the howling desert of South London"
Watson's romantic, poetic view contrasts Holmes’ logic — shows his emotional depth.
Dr. Watson
"What was I, an army surgeon with a weak leg and a weaker banking-account?"
Shows his insecurities and sense of inadequacy in contrast to Holmes.
Dr. Watson
"You really are an automaton – a calculating machine"
Watson is frustrated by Holmes’ lack of empathy — reveals their different personalities.
Mary Morstan
"A blonde young lady, small, dainty, well gloved"
Physical description reflects ideal Victorian femininity — gentle and respectable.
Mary Morstan
"She must have been more than woman if she did not feel some uneasiness"
Shows her courage — though scared, she faces the mystery.
Mary Morstan
"She was weak and helpless, shaken in mind and nerve"
Victorian gender expectations — her fragility contrasts the male leads.
Jonathan Small
"A fierce, bald, bearded man"
His intimidating physical description sets him up as a villain.
Jonathan Small
"It was not murder, but self-defence"
He justifies his crime, revealing moral ambiguity and desire for justice.
Jonathan Small
"The treasure is gone!"
Highlights the futility of his greed — a moment of despair and loss.
Inspector Goole
"We are members of one body"
Communicates the central message of social responsibility and unity.
Inspector Goole
"It's better to ask for the earth than to take it"
Criticises the greed and entitlement of the upper class.
Inspector Goole
"Fire and blood and anguish"
Foreshadows war and destruction if social injustice continues — prophetic tone.
Arthur Birling
"A man has to make his own way"
Represents capitalist individualism — contrasted by Inspector’s socialist message.
Arthur Birling
"Unsinkable, absolutely unsinkable"
Dramatic irony undermines his authority and shows his ignorance.
Arthur Birling
"The famous younger generation who know it all"
Mocks younger people, which reflects his stubbornness and outdated views.
Sybil Birling
"Girls of that class"
Reveals her prejudice and classism — looks down on the working class.
Sybil Birling
"I did nothing I'm ashamed of"
Shows her refusal to take responsibility — symbol of upper-class denial.
Sybil Birling
"Go and look for the father of the child. It's his responsibility."
Irony — she's unknowingly blaming her own son, highlighting hypocrisy.
Sheila Birling
"But these girls aren’t cheap labour – they’re people"
Shows her growing awareness and empathy — generational change.
Sheila Birling
"It frightens me the way you talk"
Rejects her parents’ lack of remorse — aligns with the Inspector’s message.
Sheila Birling
"I know I’m to blame – and I’m desperately sorry"
Accepts responsibility — represents hope for a more compassionate future.
Eric Birling
"You’re not the kind of father a chap could go to"
Reveals broken family relationships and emotional isolation.
Eric Birling
"I’m ashamed of you as well – yes both of you"
Condemns his parents’ attitude — generational conflict and moral maturity.
Eric Birling
"The fact remains that I did what I did"
Takes responsibility unlike his parents — moral development by end of play.
