GCSE English Lit Flashcards

Poetry Analysis
Added Gerald Croft to AIC.
Macbeth
"Is this a dagger which I see before me"

Summary: Macbeth's hallucination of a dagger, a symbol of his guilt and moral conflict before killing Duncan.

Context: Jacobean beliefs in the supernatural, regicide as unnatural (Divine Right of Kings), foreshadows bloodshed and Macbeth's madness. Explores appearance vs reality and ambition.

Macbeth
"I am in blood stepped in so far"

Summary: Macbeth acknowledges he is too deeply involved in murder to turn back, symbolizing his irreversible guilt and transformation into a tyrant.

Context: Recurrent blood imagery representing guilt, Jacobean beliefs on corrupting power, theme of violence begetting violence, contrasts with Lady Macbeth's initial view on guilt.

Macbeth
"Vaulting ambition"

Summary: Macbeth's metaphor for his dangerous, unchecked ambition that will lead to his downfall.

Context: Central theme of the play, connects to Jacobean beliefs about disrupting natural order (Great Chain of Being), relevant to King James I and the Gunpowder Plot, Macbeth's tragic flaw.

Lady Macbeth
"Unsex me here"

Summary: Lady Macbeth invokes spirits to remove her feminine qualities, which she sees as hindering her ambition and capacity for cruelty.

Context: Challenges Jacobean gender expectations, theme of the unnatural, foreshadows her later mental breakdown.

Lady Macbeth
"A little water clears us of this deed"

Summary: Lady Macbeth's initial belief that they can easily wash away the physical evidence and guilt of Duncan's murder.

Context: Dramatic irony (contrasts with her later hand-washing), symbolism of water and blood, theme of appearance vs reality, underestimation of psychological consequences and disruption of natural order.

Lady Macbeth
"Out, damned spot!"

Summary: Lady Macbeth's desperate hallucination during sleepwalking, trying to wash away imaginary bloodstains, symbolizing her overwhelming guilt.

Context: Contrasts with her earlier confidence ("a little water clears us"), shows her mental deterioration, links to Jacobean beliefs in divine punishment, demonstrates consequences of disrupting natural order.

Banquo
"Thou shalt get kings, though thou be none"

Summary: The witches' prophecy to Banquo that his descendants will be kings, while he won't be.

Context: Establishes historical link to King James I, creates Macbeth's motive for murder, explores fate vs free will (Banquo's restraint contrasts with Macbeth), introduces idea of legacy through bloodlines.

Banquo
"What, can the devil speak true?"

Summary: Banquo's skeptical reaction to the witches' prophecies, questioning if evil forces can tell the truth.

Context: Banquo as a moral foil to Macbeth, reflects Jacobean beliefs about witches and Satanic influence, theme of deception and ambiguous truth, foreshadows how prophecies lead to harm.

Banquo
"Fly, good Fleance, fly!"

Summary: Banquo's final words urging his son Fleance to escape the murderers.

Context: Shows Banquo's selfless character, creates dramatic tension (Fleance's escape keeps prophecy alive), marks a turning point in Macbeth's plans, foreshadows future vengeance, flatters King James I's lineage.

Macduff
"O horror, horror, horror!"

Summary: Macduff's raw, emotional reaction upon discovering King Duncan's murdered body.

Context: Establishes Macduff's loyalty and genuine shock, reinforces theme of regicide disrupting natural order, contrasts with Macbeth's feigned reactions, marks Macduff as Macbeth's future nemesis.

Macduff
"All my pretty ones?"

Summary: Macduff's heartbroken question upon learning of his family's murder.

Context: Humanizes Macduff and provides personal motivation for revenge, explores different aspects of masculinity (feeling vs acting), creates dramatic irony (audience saw the murder), solidifies Macduff as Macbeth's opponent, shows Macbeth's moral depth of depravity.

Macduff
"Turn, hell-hound, turn!"

Summary: Macduff's forceful challenge to Macbeth during their final battle.

Context: Bestial metaphor dehumanizes Macbeth, links him to evil, fulfills the witches' equivocal prophecy (Macduff was not "of woman born" in conventional sense), represents restoration of moral order, climactic moment of catharsis.

The Witches
"Fair is foul, and foul is fair"

Summary: The witches' paradoxical statement suggesting the inversion of moral values and deceptive appearances.

Context: Sets unsettling tone, introduces theme of appearance vs reality, connects to Macbeth's first line, reflects Jacobean fears of witchcraft and Satanic inversion of values, foreshadows moral corruption and downfall. Distinctive trochaic meter.

The Witches
"None of woman born shall harm Macbeth"

Summary: The witches' seemingly reassuring prophecy that gives Macbeth false confidence but contains a fatal loophole.

Context: Example of equivocation (ambiguous language), explores fate vs free will, relates to contemporary debate on equivocation, creates dramatic irony (audience learns the truth before Macbeth), shows Macbeth's dangerous overconfidence.

The Witches
"Double, double toil and trouble"

Summary: The witches' rhythmic incantation while brewing a potion, symbolizing chaos and the combining of evil.

Context: Trochaic meter distinguishes them from humans, grotesque ingredients reflect Jacobean fears of witchcraft, symbolizes how evil actions compound, occurs at play's midpoint intensifying supernatural elements, relevant to King James I's interest in witchcraft.

Sherlock Holmes
"My mind... rebels at stagnation."

Summary: Holmes describes his need for constant intellectual stimulation, explaining his reliance on cases and, at times, drugs when bored.

Context: Establishes Holmes's character and complex psychology, reflects late-Victorian anxieties about degeneration, shows the value placed on intellectual activity and productivity, sets up the need for the incoming case. Embodies scientific rationalism.

Sherlock Holmes
"You know my methods. Apply them."

Summary: Holmes directs Watson to use his logical and observational techniques in the investigation.

Context: Shows Holmes as a teacher, highlights the scientific basis of his detection, reflects Victorian values of self-improvement and rational inquiry, allows readers to follow Watson's attempts, contrasts with earlier detective methods. Reveals a rare generous side of Holmes.

Sherlock Holmes
"Detection is, or ought to be, an exact science"

Summary: Holmes's declaration of his philosophy that crime-solving should be based on precise scientific methodology.

Context: Central theme, champions Victorian scientific progress and rationalism, elevates detection to an intellectual discipline, contrasts with exotic case elements, reflects positivist philosophy. Shows potential blindness to non-rational human factors.

Dr. Watson
"An oasis of art in the howling desert of South London"

Summary: Watson's poetic description of Pondicherry Lodge, contrasting its beauty with the perceived harshness of South London.

Context: Demonstrates Watson's romantic literary style vs Holmes's precision, reflects Victorian attitudes toward working-class areas, uses orientalist imagery paralleling the case's exotic elements, provides emotional engagement for the reader, shows Watson's role as interpreter.

Dr. Watson
"What was I, an army surgeon with a weak leg and a weaker banking-account?"

Summary: Watson's self-deprecating rhetorical question expressing insecurity about proposing to Mary Morstan due to physical and financial inadequacy.

Context: Humanizes Watson, explores Victorian masculine identity (tied to physical/financial capability), injury symbolizes cost of empire, concern about money reflects Victorian marriage expectations, shows his moral character (avoiding mercenary appearance). Contrasts with Holmes's detached rationality.

Dr. Watson
"You really are an automaton – a calculating machine"

Summary: Watson's criticism of Holmes's apparent lack of emotion regarding Mary Morstan.

Context: Highlights fundamental difference between Holmes's logic and Watson's emotion, creates central character dynamic, reflects Victorian anxieties about industrialization dehumanizing people. Holmes's response justifies his detachment for objective analysis. Watson acts as a foil and bridge for the reader.

Mary Morstan
"A blonde young lady, small, dainty, well gloved"

Summary: Watson's initial physical description of Mary, establishing her as an embodiment of Victorian feminine ideals.

Context: Portrays her as a conventional love interest, contrasts with threatening elements, emphasizes Victorian propriety and modesty ("well gloved"), reflects aesthetic preference for delicate femininity, blonde color plays into racial typology (contrasting with Tonga), shows her social position (impoverished governess). Represents domestic values of empire.

Mary Morstan
"She must have been more than woman if she did not feel some uneasiness"

Summary: Watson's observation acknowledging Mary's courage but reinforcing Victorian gender expectations about female sensitivity to danger.

Context: Creates tension while maintaining femininity (fearlessness would be "unwomanly"), reflects Victorian belief that nervousness was feminine, allows her to participate while conforming to norms, makes her relatable. Shows how Conan Doyle navigates gender ideals between Gothic heroines and "New Woman."

Mary Morstan
"She was weak and helpless, shaken in mind and nerve"

Summary: Description of Mary's reaction to the treasure's disappearance, showing her physical fragility but moral strength.

Context: Reinforces Victorian feminine fragility expectations, but her distress over Thaddeus Sholto (not the money) reveals her moral superiority, allowing Watson to propose freely. Reflects Victorian class values (character over wealth). Creates dramatic tension, validates Watson's protective masculinity.

Jonathan Small
"A fierce, bald, bearded man"

Summary: Description of Small's physical appearance, establishing him as dangerous and contrasting with Holmes and Watson.

Context: Marks him as working-class/threatening, uses harsh descriptors, represents convergence of Victorian anxieties (dangerous working class, colonial returnee, disabled), links to physiognomy, missing leg symbolizes cost of imperial service. Initial impression contrasts with later moral ambiguity.

Jonathan Small
"It was not murder, but self-defence"

Summary: Small's justification for his actions, framing them as protecting the treasure he felt entitled to rather than cold-blooded murder.

Context: Reveals his complex moral position (criminal/victim), transforms him from simple villain via extended confession, explores Victorian legal/moral distinctions (murder vs self-defense), presents colonial India as morally corrupting, introduces class critique. Introduces moral relativism to the detective format.

Jonathan Small
"The treasure is gone!"

Summary: Small's exclamation revealing he discarded the Agra treasure in the Thames.

Context: Climactic twist subverting adventure ending, emphasizes moral themes over material gain, shows futility of greed, treasure becomes symbol of corruption. Thames location suggests morally compromised imperial wealth. Frees romance plot from financial implications. Punishes greed and focuses on detection process.

Inspector Goole
"We are members of one body"

Summary: The Inspector's key statement encapsulating the play's socialist message of collective responsibility.

Context: Metaphor of society as organism challenges individualist capitalism (Birling's view), inclusive language ("we") breaks class barriers, anticipates post-war welfare state/NHS (written 1945, set 1912), harm to one affects all (Eva Smith), Goole as Priestley's mouthpiece, connects to 1945 Labour reforms and desire for equality.

Inspector Goole
"It's better to ask for the earth than to take it"

Summary: Statement defending workers' rights to demand better conditions (ask) and critiquing capitalist exploitation (take).

Context: Counters Birling's view of strikers, validates Eva Smith's action, reflects Priestley's socialist sympathies, written after WWII (strengthening labor rights), contrasts Goole's plain speaking with Birlings' euphemisms, distillation of ethical principles. Connects to 1940s union power and Labour victory.

Inspector Goole
"Fire and blood and anguish"

Summary: The Inspector's ominous prophecy warning of violent consequences if society doesn't embrace collective responsibility.

Context: Concludes final speech, uses biblical/apocalyptic language, dramatic irony for audiences (WWI & WWII experienced), "fire" evokes war/hell, makes moral message unavoidable climax, transforms Eva's tragedy into social warning, structural tension leading to final revelation.

Arthur Birling
"A man has to make his own way"

Summary: Birling's declaration of his individualistic, capitalist philosophy.

Context: Embodies the play's critique of selfish individualism, gendered phrasing reflects patriarchal worldview, directly contrasts Inspector's collective responsibility message, represents pre-war mentality (written 1945, set 1912), led to social inequality (Eva Smith's exploitation), references real historical figures showing aspirational snobbery.

Arthur Birling
"Unsinkable, absolutely unsinkable"

Summary: Birling's confident, ironically incorrect assertion about the Titanic.

Context: Dramatic irony undermines Birling's credibility immediately, historical reference obvious to audience, represents his shortsightedness and inability to see danger/consequences, prepares audience to question his other statements (war, labor), Titanic symbolizes vulnerability of Birling's reputation and social order.

Arthur Birling
"The famous younger generation who know it all"

Summary: Birling's sarcastic dismissal of youth and their progressive ideas.

Context: Reveals his dismissive attitude towards social change, establishes generational conflict (outdated vs progressive values), foreshadows conflict with Eric/Sheila, resonates with post-WWII tensions between generations, structural device showing resistance to moral growth. Reveals his fundamental resistance to change and self-reflection.

Sybil Birling
"Girls of that class"

Summary: Mrs. Birling's dismissive phrase revealing her deep-seated classism and lack of empathy for working-class women.

Context: Dehumanizing language enables moral disengagement, "that" creates linguistic distance, shows how class prejudice affects her charity work (institutional power), blinds her to Eric's involvement (dramatic irony), reveals intersection of class/gender hierarchies, shows moral hypocrisy (different standard for Gerald), exemplifies language enabling exploitation.

Sybil Birling
"I did nothing I'm ashamed of"

Summary: Mrs. Birling's defiant refusal to accept responsibility for her actions concerning Eva Smith.

Context: Represents moral blindness of privileged classes, shows lack of self-awareness/ethical reflection, contrasts with Sheila's acceptance (generational divide), hypocrisy given her charity role, defense based on following procedures ("did my duty"), embodies rigid class attitudes Priestley critiques.

Sybil Birling
"Go and look for the father of the child. It's his responsibility."

Summary: Mrs. Birling's unintentionally ironic statement condemning the unknown father of Eva Smith's child, who turns out to be her son Eric.

Context: Creates dramatic tension and irony (audience knows before she does), emphasizes paternal responsibility while she denies her own, represents intersection of public morality and private hypocrisy, intensifies critique of upper-class hypocrisy, statement boomerangs back on her family, forces her to confront contradictory standards.

Sheila Birling
"But these girls aren't cheap labour – they're people"

Summary: Sheila's assertion challenging her father's dehumanizing view of workers, showing her developing social conscience.

Context: Establishes Sheila as morally educable, directly humanizes workers (counters capitalist reduction), shows recognition of individuals (vs mother's "that class"), moral awakening after admitting her role, represents hope for younger generations, challenges father's philosophy.

Sheila Birling
"It frightens me the way you talk"

Summary: Sheila's emotional reaction to her parents' denial and refusal to accept responsibility.

Context: Shows her moral growth and rejection of parents' values, emphasizes serious ethical implications, indicates genuine moral concern (not just social embarrassment), establishes generational divide (Sheila accepts responsibility), represents completed moral journey, her fear links to potential broader social catastrophe (Inspector's prophecy).

Sheila Birling
"I know I'm to blame – and I'm desperately sorry"

Summary: Sheila's early admission of responsibility and genuine remorse for her actions.

Context: Establishes her capacity for moral growth (contrasting parents), first family member to respond appropriately, willingness to face uncomfortable truths sets her apart, marks beginning of transformation from spoiled child, language lacks euphemisms (direct), shows how privilege enabled casual cruelty. Raises expectations for others' responses.

Gerald Croft
"We're respectable citizens and not criminals."

Summary: Gerald tries to defend himself and the Birlings, claiming their social class makes them trustworthy.

Context: Shows Gerald’s upper-class attitude and belief that rich people can’t be guilty. Priestley uses this to highlight the arrogance of the elite and their refusal to accept blame, even when their actions harm others.

Gerald Croft
"I didn’t feel about her as she felt about me."

Summary: Gerald admits he used Eva Smith emotionally, even though she had real feelings for him.

Context: Shows the imbalance in their relationship. Gerald is honest, but still downplays the damage he caused. Priestley uses this to expose how powerful men often exploit vulnerable women.

Gerald Croft
"Everything’s all right now, Sheila. What about this ring?"

Summary: Gerald offers Sheila the engagement ring again, hoping they can forget what happened.

Context: Gerald shows he hasn’t changed, while Sheila has. Priestley uses this to show how the younger generation can grow, but only if they take responsibility. Gerald represents those who want to ignore their faults and move on.

Eric Birling
"You're not the kind of father a chap could go to"

Summary: Eric's accusation revealing the lack of emotional connection and guidance in his relationship with his father.

Context: Reveals dysfunctional family dynamics masked by propriety, formal language shows emotional detachment, broken relationship parallels social disconnections (Birling failing workers), contributes to Eric's alcoholism/behavior, challenges Victorian ideal of father as moral leader, shows how father's unavailability contributed to Eric's choices, links private dysfunction to public responsibility.

Eric Birling
"I'm ashamed of you as well – yes both of you"

Summary: Eric's condemnation of his parents, showing his moral awakening and rejection of their values.

Context: Role reversal (son judging parents), emphasizes generational shift and hope for change, repetition shows newfound confidence, maintains responsibility even if Inspector fraudulent (genuine growth), redirects shame onto parents, links to socialist message of rejecting established hierarchies/prejudices.

Eric Birling
"The fact remains that I did what I did"

Summary: Eric's straightforward acceptance of responsibility for his actions, cutting through his parents' evasions.

Context: Demonstrates moral growth (contrasts parents), simple declarative sentence emphasizes reality of actions regardless of Inspector's identity, focuses on moral essence (harm caused), clarity emerges despite marginalized position, alongside Sheila creates concluding moral division, prioritizes actions/consequences over intentions, simplicity suggests authenticity vs parents' rhetoric.
